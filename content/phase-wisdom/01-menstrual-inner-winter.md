# Menstrual · The Inner Winter

There is a reason your body wants the blanket. A reason even the coffee doesn't help. A reason the social calendar looks like too much, and the inside of your home looks like relief. This is your winter. Every cycle, you get one.

Ayurveda calls this phase *rajah-srava* — the flow of *rajas*, the outward-downward current of menstrual blood. Modern biology calls it the menstrual phase. They are describing the same body, using different vocabularies, and they agree on one thing: this is a time of release, not of building.

## The body

Your estrogen and progesterone are at their lowest of the entire cycle. The corpus luteum from last month's ovulation has dissolved. The endometrial lining — which spent the last two weeks thickening in case of pregnancy — has lost its hormonal signal to stay. It sheds. You bleed.

Prostaglandins, local hormone-like compounds, are elevated. They cause the uterine muscle to contract (that's the cramp) and they can also affect the gut (hello, period poops) and inflammation throughout the body. Iron loss through blood flow means your oxygen-carrying capacity dips — explaining why the stairs feel steeper. Core body temperature drops. Sleep often shifts. Many people notice vivid dreams.

In one sentence: your body is doing demanding, tissue-level work *while also* running on low hormonal fuel. It's no wonder you feel what you feel.

## What the classical texts say

In Ayurveda, the menstrual phase belongs to *Vata dosha*. Specifically, to *apana vayu* — the current of Vata that governs all downward and outward movement in the body: urination, defecation, menstruation, childbirth, ejaculation. The *Charaka Samhita Sharira Sthana* describes apana vayu as seated in the pelvic cavity, and identifies healthy menstruation as one of its normal expressions. When apana is flowing well, menses arrive on time and leave cleanly. When apana is disturbed — by suppression of urges, over-stimulation, cold food, harsh emotional climate, or constipation — the cycle expresses the disturbance as pain, clots, irregularity, or suppression.

*Artava* (the menstrual fluid) is classified as an *upadhatu* — a subordinate tissue — formed in the metabolism of the body's nutritive essence. Classical Ayurveda has two views on its precise lineage: *Charaka Samhita* and the majority modern consensus place it as an upadhatu of *rasa dhatu* (the plasma/nutrient tissue); some *Sushruta* commentaries relate it to *rakta dhatu* (the blood tissue). Either way, the point stands: it is not waste. It is refined, cyclical, and sacred — the body takes the same raw material it uses to build, and releases it so the next cycle can begin fresh.

In the classical three-phase model, the menstrual week is called ***Rajahsravakaala*** (*rajah-srava* = "flow of the menstrual essence") — one of three phases of *Rituchakra* (the cycle), the other two being *Ritukaala* (the fertile window that follows) and *Rituvyatitakaala* (the preparatory phase before the next menses).

The *Ashtanga Hridaya* goes further and prescribes a specific regimen for menstruating women — *rajasvala-charya*. The core instructions, repeated across classical Ayurveda, are strikingly practical: rest; eat warm, soft, easily digested food; sleep on the floor or a simple bed; abstain from strenuous exertion; avoid loud argument or grief; do not bathe excessively hot; do not sleep during the day. In short: withdraw gently, and let the body do its work.

## What this means for today

You do not need to sleep on the floor. You don't need to cancel your job. But the ancient principle is worth modernizing:

1. **Warmth over cold.** Warm foods (kitchari, soups, spiced milk). Warm water, not iced. A hot water bottle on the belly or lower back often relieves cramps more than paracetamol.
2. **Less exertion, not zero.** High-intensity interval training is typically too much in these days. Gentle walks, restorative yoga, *savasana* — yes.
3. **Fewer inputs.** Screens, notifications, social plans. If your body is releasing, your attention can too.
4. **Oily grounding.** A slow warm-sesame-oil abdomen massage before a bath invites *apana* to flow unobstructed. (Avoid intense abhyanga on cramping days — light touch only.)
5. **Iron-rich nourishment.** Cooked spinach, dates, jaggery, sesame seeds, lentils — replenish what's leaving.
6. **Let sleep be long.** Period sleep is different. Honor it.

This is a quiet week. The quiet is the medicine.

## When to see a doctor

Please consult a physician (don't rely on the app) if you experience:

- Bleeding that soaks through a pad or tampon every hour for more than two hours.
- Severe pain that ibuprofen or paracetamol does not relieve.
- Periods lasting longer than 8 days.
- A missed period when pregnancy is possible — take a test.
- Bleeding between periods, or spotting after sex.
- A sudden change in pattern from what's normal for you (much heavier, much shorter, much more painful).

These can signal anything from fibroids to endometriosis to thyroid imbalance to a hormone issue that deserves proper evaluation. Your cycle is information. A good doctor reads it.
