# The LH Surge · Your Body's Ovulation Trigger

Of all the hormonal events of your cycle, the LH surge is perhaps the most dramatic. For most of the month, LH (luteinizing hormone) runs at a baseline of ~5-10 mIU/mL. Over about 24 hours in mid-cycle, it rises **ten-fold** — 50-100 mIU/mL — and then falls again almost as fast. Ovulation happens within 36 hours of the surge's onset.

This is the biological trigger that releases an egg.

## What LH is

A hormone produced by the **anterior pituitary gland** at the base of the brain. Throughout your follicular phase, LH hums along at low levels, just enough to keep the ovaries supplied. Then, suddenly, it surges.

## What triggers the surge

The trigger is **your own estrogen**.

For most of the cycle, rising estrogen *suppresses* pituitary LH (negative feedback). But when estrogen crosses a threshold and *stays above it for ~48 hours* — as happens when a mature dominant follicle is producing peak estradiol — the hypothalamus flips modes. Estrogen now *stimulates* LH release (positive feedback). The pituitary lets go of an enormous pulse of LH.

This switch is unique in endocrinology. Nowhere else does the same signal shift from inhibitor to activator.

## What the surge does

Within 36 hours of the surge beginning:
1. The **dominant follicle ruptures** and releases its mature egg into the fallopian tube — this is ovulation proper
2. The ruptured follicle transforms into the **corpus luteum**, which starts producing progesterone
3. Cervical mucus changes (from stretchy/clear to thick/scant within a day or two)
4. BBT rises 0.3-0.5°C within 24-48 hours as progesterone takes over

## Can you feel the LH surge?

Sometimes, yes:
- **Sharp mid-cycle pain** on one side (*mittelschmerz*) — usually on the side of the ovulating ovary; can last a few hours to a day
- **Cervical mucus** becomes noticeably stretchy and clear ("egg-white" texture) — this is the peak fertility signal
- **Libido surge** — biologically timed; driven by both estrogen peak and the small testosterone bump that accompanies the LH surge
- **Mild breast tenderness**
- **Heightened sense of smell** (documented in research)
- **Social confidence and energy** — the cognitive clarity many people associate with "feeling their best"

Not everyone feels all of these. Many people feel nothing and only know they've ovulated retrospectively when their BBT rises.

## How to detect it reliably

**Ovulation Predictor Kits (OPKs)** — urine-strip tests for LH. Widely available in Indian pharmacies (Velocit, Pregahome, Clearblue). Start testing around day 10 of your cycle. A positive indicates the surge has begun and ovulation will occur within 12-36 hours.

Tips:
- **Test in the afternoon**, not first morning urine — LH surge often begins after waking, so a morning test may miss the early hours
- **Test daily** starting day 10-11 if your cycle is regular ~28 days; earlier if your cycle is shorter
- **Don't trust a single test** — the surge window is short; missing a day of testing can miss the surge entirely
- **Baseline variable** — some people have naturally high LH (e.g., with PCOS), making OPKs less reliable

**Digital tracking** (Fitbit, Oura, dedicated devices like Mira) combines BBT + LH measurement for more reliable detection.

**Cervical mucus observation** (Billings method, Fertility Awareness Method) — free, trainable, requires consistency

**Ayurveda's classical description** is in *Sushruta Samhita*: the woman in *ritu-kala* has healthy vagina, uterus, and ovum — and is capable of conception. Classical Indian women were taught to notice the body's signals; we've since forgotten.

## When to see a doctor

- **No detectable LH surge across 3+ cycles** despite regular testing → possible anovulation
- **Persistently elevated LH with normal FSH** on a day-3 blood test → can suggest PCOS
- **Trying to conceive unsuccessfully for 12 months** (6 months if over 35) → workup
- **Very short cycles (<21 days)** → possible premature ovulation or luteal phase defect
