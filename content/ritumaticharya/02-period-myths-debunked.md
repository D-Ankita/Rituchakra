# Period Myths · What's Ayurveda, What's Culture, What's Neither

Every Indian girl grows up with rules about her period. Some come from loving mothers and grandmothers. Some from priests. Some from the village. Some from misunderstood fragments of Ayurveda.

Let's separate them.

**Ground rule:** *Ayurveda's* classical medical tradition (Charaka, Sushruta, Ashtanga Hridaya) differs significantly from *Dharmashastra* and later *smriti* traditions that came after. Both have been called "what the scriptures say" at various points, but they are different texts, different purposes, different authorities.

## Myth 1 · "Don't wash your hair during your period"

**What the classical texts say:** *Ashtanga Hridaya* advises avoiding *cold* bathing during menses because cold aggravates vata, which can disrupt flow. That's it.

**What it became:** A blanket prohibition on any bathing, eventually including hair washing.

**The reality:** You can absolutely wash your hair. Use **warm water** — not cold, not scalding hot. Done mindfully, washing warmly is consistent with the classical intent (preserve warmth, support flow). Don't wash in freezing water and then sit in a draft — that's the only thing the text objects to.

## Myth 2 · "Don't enter the kitchen"

**What the classical texts say:** Nothing. *Rajasvala-charya* does not mention the kitchen.

**What it became:** A religious-cultural rule tied to later concepts of ritual impurity affecting food purity.

**The reality:** You can cook. You can touch the rotis. You can feed your family. Your period does not spoil food.

**A gentler version that IS in the spirit of Ayurveda:** if you're exhausted, let someone else cook for a day. Not because you're impure — because you need rest. The form is the same; the reasoning is opposite.

## Myth 3 · "Don't enter the temple / worship / touch the idols"

**What the classical texts say:** Nothing in Ayurveda. This is a religious practice with complex origins.

**The honest history:** Some Hindu temple traditions do restrict menstruating women. This is a religious rule, not a medical or Ayurvedic one. Different schools of Hinduism treat this differently. Many modern practitioners and reform movements reject it.

**The reality for you:** This is a religious question, not a health or Ayurveda question. You get to decide how to navigate it based on your own relationship with faith, family, and practice.

## Myth 4 · "Don't touch plants — they'll die"

**What the classical texts say:** Nothing. Pure folk belief.

**The reality:** Your tulsi plant, your jasmine, your money plant — all fine. Water them.

## Myth 5 · "Don't touch pickles — they'll spoil"

**What the classical texts say:** Nothing.

**The reality:** Your menstruation has no effect on pickle fermentation. Pickles spoil because of moisture, salt balance, and temperature — not because a menstruating woman was nearby.

## Myth 6 · "Don't wash your face cold / drink cold water / eat ice cream"

**What the classical texts say:** ✅ **Partially true.** *Cold* things aggravate *vata*, which is already dominant during menses. Classical Ayurveda does recommend warm water and warm food.

**The reality:** This one has classical basis. Preferring warm water and warm food during menses is well-supported by the *rajasvala-charya*. Skip iced beverages, smoothies, raw salads, ice cream if you can. This isn't superstition — it's pragmatic comfort.

**Caveat:** If you're in peak summer and craving cold, a little is fine. Don't turn Ayurvedic wisdom into rigid fear.

## Myth 7 · "Don't have sex during your period"

**What the classical texts say:** ✅ **Yes, classical Ayurveda recommends abstaining.** The reasoning: apana is in its outward/downward flow; sexual activity can disturb this.

**The reality:** Modern views vary. If you and your partner prefer to abstain for the classical reason, that's fine. If you're comfortable, that's fine too. This isn't a moral question — it's a preference one. Medical safety: use a condom regardless (STI protection is unchanged; pregnancy is possible but less likely).

## Myth 8 · "Don't exercise at all"

**What the classical texts say:** ✅ **Partially true.** Classical texts advise against *strenuous* exercise during menses. Not against all movement.

**The reality:** **Gentle movement is good.** Walking, stretching, yoga (avoiding inversions and deep twists on heavy days), pilates at low intensity. Skip HIIT, heavy lifting, long runs.

## Myth 9 · "Your hair will stop growing" / "Your skin will darken" / "Your eyes will weaken"

**What the classical texts say:** Nothing.

**The reality:** These are superstitions. Your hair keeps growing. Your skin is fine. Your eyes are fine.

## Myth 10 · "You're impure / polluting / shouldn't be near people"

**What the classical texts say:** Ayurveda's framing of menstruation is of a **cleansing** process — the body releasing what it no longer needs. The word *rajas* (menstrual blood) carries connotations of *dynamism and movement*, not pollution.

**What it became:** Later Dharmashastra and social texts introduced the concept of ritual impurity (*ashuddha* / *sutak*). These texts served different purposes (law, ritual) than the medical texts.

**The reality:** You are not polluting. You are not dirty. You are menstruating. This is a physiological process, not a moral one.

Some people grew up in homes where this was deeply felt. Unlearning it takes time. Gentle on yourself — and gentle on the elders who taught it to you. They received it; they didn't invent it.

---

## Two principles to take with you

1. **Warmth is classical. Exertion-avoidance is classical. Impurity is not.**
2. **Your period is a physiological process, not a moral or religious one.** Any rules that frame it as impurity, restriction, or shame — feel free to let go, in whatever way honors your family and faith without harming your dignity.

The classical Ayurvedic regimen, correctly understood, is tender. It says: *your body is doing something demanding this week. Rest. Eat warm. Move gently.* That's the whole message.
