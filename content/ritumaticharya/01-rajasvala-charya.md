# Rajasvala-charya · The Classical Menstrual Regimen

*Rajasvala* means "she who has the menstrual flow." *Charya* means "conduct" or "regimen." *Rajasvala-charya* is Ayurveda's explicit, classical guide to how a woman should live during her period — specifically during ***Rajahsravakaala***, the first of three phases of the classical *Rituchakra* (the Ayurvedic term for menstrual cycle). The other two phases are *Ritukaala* (the fertile window, roughly days 6-16) and *Rituvyatitakaala* (the preparatory phase before the next menses).

Here's what's striking: read it carefully, and you find a regimen that looks remarkably like **self-care for anyone recovering from demanding physical work**. Rest. Warm food. Less exertion. Quiet company. No harsh activities.

That's it. It's not about impurity. It's about supporting the body's most physiologically demanding week.

## What the classical texts actually say

Drawing directly from *Ashtanga Hridaya · Sharira Sthana 1.24* (the most detailed single source) and *Charaka · Sharira Sthana 2*:

### The menstruating woman should:
- **Sleep on a simple, low bed** — not luxurious or overly soft
- **Eat warm, unctuous (slightly oily/fatty), easily-digestible food in small amounts**
- **Rest more than usual**
- **Stay in a calm environment** — minimize emotional turbulence
- **Keep warm** — avoid cold air, cold food, cold water

### The menstruating woman should avoid:
- **Strenuous physical exertion** — heavy lifting, intense exercise, long walks
- **Daytime sleep** — not because sleep is bad, but because it aggravates certain doshas in specific ways (though some modern Ayurvedic writers question this one for pitta constitutions)
- **Emotional overwhelm** — loud arguments, grief, shock
- **Cold food and cold bathing** — the qualities that aggravate vata
- **Sexual activity** — to honor the body's cleansing process
- **Anointing the body with heavy oils** (deep abhyanga) — the body is already in an outward-movement state; deep massage can intensify flow
- **Wearing tight, binding clothing**

## Why these rules — the doshic logic

The menstrual phase is **vata-dominant**. *Apana vayu* — the downward-flowing current — is already active, carrying out its most physiologically demanding task. The classical regimen's entire purpose is to **not disturb this natural process**.

Each rule maps to a doshic principle:
- **Cold food** aggravates vata → suppresses flow, causes cramping
- **Heavy exertion** aggravates vata → exhausts the body, can intensify cramping
- **Emotional turbulence** aggravates vata → disrupts sleep, worsens mood-hormonal interplay
- **Strong physical pressure** (deep massage, sexual activity) can disturb apana's downward movement
- **Warm, light, simple food** supports vata without burdening agni (digestion)

It's pragmatic. It's kind. It's centered on the body's actual state.

## What the classical texts did NOT say

The following restrictions appear in later Indian cultural practice but are **not in the classical Ayurvedic texts** — or are present in forms we'd now consider distorted:

- ❌ *"A menstruating woman is impure"* — Ayurveda's framing is of a sacred, cleansing process, not a polluting one
- ❌ *"A menstruating woman cannot enter the kitchen"* — no classical Ayurvedic basis; this is a later cultural/religious practice
- ❌ *"A menstruating woman cannot worship"* — again, cultural/religious accretion, not Ayurvedic
- ❌ *"She must sit separately, eat separately"* — not in Ayurveda
- ❌ *"She must not touch plants or flowers"* — not in Ayurveda
- ❌ *"She cannot wash her hair"* — more complex: classical texts advise against *cold* bathing, which has since been distorted into "no bathing at all"

These restrictions grew from later social practices and sometimes from religious systems (Dharmashastra texts), not from Ayurveda's medical/wellness tradition. It matters to separate them, because many Indian women grew up with these cultural rules framed as "what Ayurveda says." They aren't.

## The modern adaptation

Applied today, *rajasvala-charya* looks like this:

### For days 1-3 of your period:
- **Go to bed earlier than usual.** 9:30-10 pm if possible.
- **Eat warm food.** Kitchari, dal-rice, soups, warm rotis, cooked vegetables. Skip smoothies, salads, iced drinks.
- **Reduce exercise intensity.** Walks, gentle yoga, restorative poses. Save the HIIT and heavy lifting for follicular.
- **Limit intense social engagement.** The 3-hour wedding sangeet can be endured in follicular; on day 2 you can RSVP quietly no.
- **Use warm water.** Hot shower fine. Warm-water compress on belly for cramps. Warm (not hot) tea instead of cold water.
- **Wear soft, comfortable clothing.** Cotton, loose, warm.
- **Protect sleep.** Phone off an hour before bed, slow evening, minimal stimulation.

### What to be gentle with, not rigid about:
- **Work:** most women don't have the luxury of taking 3 days off. Do your work, but scale back what you volunteer for. Set internal expectations lower.
- **Hair washing:** wash in warm water if you want to. You are not ritually unclean.
- **Kitchen / cooking:** fully permitted by Ayurveda.
- **Sex:** classical regimen recommends abstaining. Modern take: your choice, based on how your body feels.
- **Exercise:** not zero. Gentle.
- **Socializing:** conservative, not absent.

## What this is really about

The *charya* is not about restriction. It's about recognition — that this week, your body is doing more, and asking the world of you to be less.

Modern life doesn't give us that space. Most women menstruate while in meetings, parenting, commuting, and pretending nothing's happening. Classical Ayurveda quietly insists: *this matters. It is worth planning for. The body is worth honoring.*

## What to take from the classical text

Three principles that transfer directly to your life:

1. **Warmth and simplicity.** Warm food. Warm bath. Warm presence.
2. **Reduce, don't eliminate.** You don't have to stop living. Just soften.
3. **This is cyclical.** Every month. Every cycle. Plan for it the way you plan for Monday mornings.
